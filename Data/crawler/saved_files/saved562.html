Although 3.Nf3 is the most often played move, also playable are 3.Bc4 (Bishop's Gambit), 3.Be2 (Lesser Bishop's gambit), Nc3 (Keres Gambit), 3.d4 (Steinitz gambit), and 3.Qf3 (Breyer Gambit - and this is better than it looks. Good advice here, Joe. The problem is to find a line that is sound enough, doesn't get played all that often, and is not tactical too early. With the White pieces, I would suggest the King's Indian Attack. But play it as though you were starting a Ruy Lopez: 1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 Nc6 3.g3(!) Nf6 4.Nc3. White has a fine, sound game. soon will come Bg2 and 0-0, then maybe d3, and then White can think about developing a K-side attack by Nh4 or Ne1, then f4. A nice, fairly simple opening behind which lies aggressive intentions.
kenneth_fiore_3 (2113) on 28-Feb-16:
If you have an opening catastrophe, as you did here, resignation is appropriate against all but the weakest opponents. There is nothing to learn against any opponent, but the weakest ones need to practice finishing such games. Main lines are main lines for a reason, but that game doesn't prove that you must stick to main lines. It does prove that you need to know the main lines if you play the KGA. If you couldn't play anything other than main lines chess would be painfully dull. Just as in the main lines, if you enter sidelines, you need to be prepared, and you need to be tactically sound, which playing d3 certainly was not. My advice would be to play an opening that requires less preparation than the King's Gambit. As archduke says below, try the King's Indian Attack. You will get a very playable position. I actually do this myself. I play the Queen's Indian Attack aka know as Larsen's Opening. I started when I was a 1200 and won a game against an 1800 back in 1974. I still play it today and it still works now, even though my opponents here are much stronger players.
teardrop34 (2191) on 28-Feb-16:
Gambit lines are not played very much at the 2000+ levels for a very good reason...they have been proven to be inferior at this level of play between equal level strength players. I occasionally pull one or 2 out but I have other ideas that I whip out as surprise value Joe but never along the lines of a King's gambit. The Indian family of openings is a great system to study as kenneth_fiore_3 points out, but my advise is just continue to play around with openings until you find your niche. Find what works best for you, then master it. What works for one player may not be what works best for another. The principles are there to give you a guide to what you need as a foundation for what makes a good opening sequence. Now you have to find a sequence that you are most comfortable with, that works best for you in any situation. That's not something any of us can help you with. That's something you have to discover on your own. Good luck.
