jkarp (1319) on 05-Feb-16:
But, better, however, was 3.Nc3 Yes, you haven't lost anything, but the problem, as I saw, about, two years ago is the same. One of your center pawns is blocked. In other words, in addition to the e pawn, you need the d pawn to control the center, as well. By playing this, it's blocked, as now, you can't advance the d pawn. Whereas, were Nc3 played, you still defend and develop, whilst, you still have the opportunity to advance the d pawn. An important thing to remember is that center control plays an important role. So, it's not just about development, but trying to contest the center is also important.

archduke_piccolo (2206) on 06-Feb-16:
There were a number of ways of dealing with this pin: [A] ducking with the Queen, as here; [B] playing 7.h3, which would force the bishop back along the diagonal whence it came, as 7...Bh4 8.g4 traps and wins the bishop for 2 pawns. [C] Playing 7.Be2, which would free the queen to move without fear of the exchange of f3 doubling the pawns. ++++++++++++ Having said all that, the choice White makes is worth experimenting with at least. The doubled pawns aren't especially weak, and the open g-file might yet become useful for White. There can be a downside, though. If Black does get an attack going, it will not be easy to bring defensive assets past the doubled pawns to help the King.
