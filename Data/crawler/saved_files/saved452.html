archduke_piccolo (2206) on 24-Apr-19:
When your opponent offers a piece like this (the loose bishop on c3) it is quite a good idea to see if there is anything behind it. Don't trust your opponent! If you don't see anything behind it, then take the material. You'll learn more that way than if you decline the material on the assumption there it is the bait in a trap. Do that too often, and your opponent will learn that he can impose on you with unsound sacrifices. Here it was understandable that 15...Bxc3 would look dangerous in the light of 16.Qf7ch, but after 16...Kh8, white has no follow-up, and his a1-Rook is 'en prise'. +++++ But here's another complication in a position that has become quite complicated: 15.... c6(!?). Now if White were to play 16.Qxf7ch Kh1, suddenly the White Queen would also be in danger from 17...Re7, as she would have no safe move. White would have to retreat to save his knight, but that would at least save his piece.
