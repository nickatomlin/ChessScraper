jkarp (1319) on 21-Sep-15:
Yes, that is a good moral. No, I don't agree that if it's even, don't exchange. That depends on the position. But, the more important lessons to learn are first, it is important to develop your pieces, control the center, and get the K to safety. Yes, this is similar to what I have seen in some of those GM games. So, for example, white kept pushing his pawns, moved the same piece several times unnecessarily, and brought the Q out early, when the time could and should've been used to get his other pieces out and castle. In fact, by bringing the Q out as early as he did, which was, on his eighth move, you were able to get a discover attack so that the only way to have protected it was by retreating. This would've cost white extra time, which you'd be able to use for once he'd retreat, you'd be able to develop another piece right then and that. So, that alone, gave you noticeable advantage, from the start. The second lesson which is also important is being aware of threats made by the opponent. However, that's essential for both sides. That's because you played 4...Nc4, overlooking that your B was en-prise. By playing this, he won a piece for a pawn. But, then he made a bigger mistake by playing 10.Bb4 after bringing his Q out too early because you had a nasty discover attack on that. So, by moving there, as opposed to, retreating, he lost his Q. It went downhill from there. Remembering this will help both you improve and build.
