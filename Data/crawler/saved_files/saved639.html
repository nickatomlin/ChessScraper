Before you attack, consider how many pieces are threatening a square from both sides of the board and the associated material value. Analyzing the square d5 after the move 3... d5, white is threatening with the e4 pawn and the LSB on c4 (worth 1 and 3 points, respectively). To completely firm up the position, the black queen is glaring at the d5 square as well. Now, if there is an exchange of pieces initiated by white, white loses material points. Relative Value of pieces: Queen = 9 points Rook = 5 points Bishop = 3 points Knight = 3 points Pawn = 1 points

russ20124 (1200) on 22-Jan-16:
The feeling you have of "starting all over again" is because you lost time ("tempo") in with the earlier bishop move. As white, you always get the first move and black is on defense. When you moved your bishop twice, that allows black to "catch up" and take the initiative. You are now on defense. As a rule of thumb, it's always best to avoid moving any piece twice in the opening portion of the game. With respect to the current move, I might consider moving 6. d4 directly instead of 6. c3. This puts white more cleanly in the center and allows your DSB additional scope (c1-c6 diagonal). In addition, c3 denies the white queen side knight a logical space to move. The white queen side pieces will be somewhat cramped up for the remainder of the game unless your opponent allows you to develop. If you look at the previous bishop move white made, you can safely move your pawn to d4 for the same reasons black was able to move to d5. White has a knight and queen guarding d4, whereas black only has a knight threatening. The e file has no black pawn to move forward in defense and the black knight at c6 is blocking the only pawn defender. Note you are prepared to castle in the near future.
