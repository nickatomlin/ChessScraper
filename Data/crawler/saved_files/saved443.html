Objectively speaking, I agree with Kenneth, but there are subjective considerations that qualify my agreement. One is the development of a tactical imagination, and that is best achieved by placing a load upon it. GM Paul Keres (of whom I am still pretty much an admirer) recommended playing 'unsound' gambits by way of exercising the mind. If you can find a copy, I recommend Fred Reinfeld's book on Keres's games. The first dozen are correspondence games from the 1930s. They are most entertaining to play through...

archduke_piccolo (2206) on 11-Jul-16:
Here I somewhat diverge from Kenneth's view. The f7-square is not the only target, but the whole light square complex in the centre. Having said that the move has a very serious tactical downside that White needed to be aware of. Look at the b4-knight. It strikes at the c2-square, which is defended by the Queen. Were Black now to play 9...Bg4, it would be attacking the f3-knight, which is defended... by the Queen. Black would be threatening ...Bxf3; Qxf3 Nxc2ch; ...Nxa1. The only good defence, then, would be 10.Be2.
