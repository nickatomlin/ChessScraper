archduke_piccolo (2206) on 31-Mar-16:
This is a sensible enough move, though rather cautious. 9.Nxe5(!) was still quite playable, and would have been the move I would have expected here. +++++++++++++ But there is a spectacular move that looks very promising for White: 9.Bf7ch!? ... Now, this is not easy to imagine, let alone calculate, but let's check it out. Black's immediate reply is forced: 9...Kxf7 10.Nxe5ch ... and now: [A] 10...Ke8 11.Qh5ch! g6 {Not ...Ng6; 12.Nxg6, winning a rook} 12.Qf3 Qc8 {The only other way to avert checkmate is to give up a knight: 12...Nf5, 13.exf5 Bxb5 14.fxg6 Qe7 15.Qa8} 13.Qf7ch Kd8 14.Qf6 Rg8 15.Nf7ch Ke8 16. Nbd6ch cxd6 17.Nxd6ch Kd8 18.Nxc8, and Black is left with a very mangled position. [B] 10...Ke6 or ...Kf6 both lead to a forced mate for White (I'll leave you to work them out) +++++++ Unfortunately, there is a move that spoils the party for White: 10...Kg8 11.Qf3 h6 {White was threatening mate, but 11...Qe8 12.Nxc7 is great for White} 12.Nf7 Qc8 13.Nxc7 Qxc7 14.Nxh8 Qc4 {hoping to discourage White from pressing home the attack by the latent mate threat at e2, whilst at the same time protecting f7.}. This line seems to leave White with insufficient attack. ++++ So White was right not to sacrifice, but it sometimes helps to explore these lines, for two reasons: 1. Practising calculating complicated play (the ability to do this comes only with practice, but its immediate benefit is to reduce instances of short-range oversights); 2. This sort of thing can give you a lot of information about a position. Having rejected 9.Bf7ch? you are more likely to see the benefits of 9.Nxe5!, with just about a winning game for White! 9...Qc8 (there are no other safe ways to prevent the threatened checkmate) 10.Bf7ch Kd8 11.Nd6!! cxd6 {forced} 12.Bxb6 Qc7 13.Qxd6!! ... See how White exploits the pin on the Black queen instead of taking it straight off. White should win from such a position.
