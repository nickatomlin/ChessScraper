 By moving the knight out and back again, White loses two tempi with Black reaping the benefit. 'Admitting a mistake' in this way shows flexibility of mind, and, every now and then one has no alternative. But it is far more often better to look for some way to extract value, even from a poor move. As it happens, 18.Nf4 was not so bad a move at all. White could now have played 19.Ne6, forcing the exchange of knights on e6 on account of the Q-R fork. Play might have gone 19.Ne6 Nxe6 20.Bxe6 Re5 21.Bxf7ch!? ... (Possibly unnecessary) 21...Rxf7 22.Qxa4. Balanced against White's extra two pawns would be Black's more active game, so the outcome would be uncertain!

archduke_piccolo (2206) on 06-Aug-17:
White had the edge for a good part of the early stages of the game, despite a less-than-optimal arrangement of pieces and pawn in the centre. Attempting to unravel the tangle proved to be mistimed, and White was thrown onto the defensive, What really compromised the game, though, was overlooking a number of 'one-move' and 'two-move' shots. When making a move, one must always seek out what the enemy is likely to do in reply. If you give 'check' will he move the King out of check, or capture the checking piece? And when your adversary makes a move, ask yourself: 'What is he about? What (if anything) is he threatening? Is he threatening to take something right off, or maybe to play a fork, or skewer, or pin or a double attack of some sort...
