 Then, he said, "I shouldn't have asked." But, this is where I explained where he went wrong. I told him that I gave that Rook for a reason. In this case, my Knight and Queen worked well together to deliver the mate. He said, "Oh, I see that." Then, I said that taking is what allowed me to do it. Better was to protect with your Queen. By that, I meant that best was 7...Qe7, as now, my N is pinned, which compromises my position, instead. Even if 7...Qe7, 8.Kxf2, he can still take back with 8...Qxe5. Not only doesn't he lose anything, but I remain a pawn down. So, I explained that the lesson to be learned is, "to take is a mistake". Therefore, it's not a good idea to take something just because you can. Instead, ask yourself what danger allies ahead, and if I could mate afterwards, like this. As sometimes, there is a very good reason for giving something away, like an immediate mate or win of material.

archduke_piccolo (2206) on 12-Sep-16:
There is some good advice there, Joe. But I think Kenneth might be overstating the case. It rather depends on whom you are playing against. I wouldn't expect you to 'master the reams of analysis' in these types of openings; I wouldn't expect me to master them neither. Yet I have successfully negotiated them on occasion against strong opposition. The trick is to avoid opening traps, and there, some study into those is well worth a look. The most well known is the Noah's Ark Trap in the Ruy Lopez - a motif you have seen before. Another that crops up is the well known and well-named 'poisoned pawn' - the ill-considered grab of the b-pawn. There are plenty of others.... all waiting hungrily.
