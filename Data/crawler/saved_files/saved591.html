As some of us know, I'm very addicted to GM annotations, especially since GM Damien Lemos gave advise to study games between masters on a regular basis. To make a long story, it started back in July of 2014 while being mentored. 6 months later, I started studying them again because GM Susan Polgar showed them to her viewers. Shortly after that, it stopped. But, that was, until two weeks ago when archduke_piccolo copied and pasted a link involving three annotations. Unfortunately, I could only study one because the rest didn't start until the 17th move. Therefore, after studying that one, I decided to look into other annotations as I'll be playing through when my time on the computer is limited. I had a hard time deciding which one. However, my decision was made to google annotations from GM Davorin Kuljasevic as he is my favorite annotator with, by far, more variations from him then any other annotated game that I've analyzed. It's also important to note that it's the first time, that I found an annotation by googling it since this, to my knowing, wasn't posted. With that being said, the annotator was Davorin and this is what he called the annotation which is why I put the title in quotes. I hope you enjoy it.

 [A controversial decision. Of course, Black is looking to get some counterplay, but generally opening up the position favors the side that holds the initiative. Strictly strategically speaking, White should be happy with this pawn exchange as in such positions he is usually the one that tries to push a2?a4 in order to create a pawn weakness on Black's queenside (either b5 or a6). With his last move Black has done this job for him and now he always needs to be careful about the a-pawn, which could prove especially weak in all sorts of endgames.] [It might have been more prudent for him to sit tight with a neutral move such as 25...Bh4 but then again, how many people (and engines!) would forego an opportunity to finally get some counterplay?]
