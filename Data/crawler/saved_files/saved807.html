 One of the more interesting modern openings to study, the Grünfeld Defence is one to truly test one's abilities. It is very active and tactical due to the open nature of the position. Naturally, there is the danger that White's big center will lead to a strong attack. Yet by sidestepping the pawn steamroller and attacking it, Black can feel relatively secure. White may have the center, but Black can chip away at it from the supports by playing ...Bg4, moving the Knight from f6, and acting on the Queenside. Enough small talk though, on with the action!

damalfi (1742) on 30-Sep-15:
It seems that in white's agenda the attack goes through Qb3-Bc4-Nf3-Ng5, taking advantage of the possible d5-d6 (sac it to open the way to f7). Black's attack on d4 must be explained: the real white problem is that d4 is pinned while c3 is undefended (d5? Bxb3+, forking a R in the process). White must find the way to defend this pawn AND attack at the same time. Qb3 seems to be the perfect move for it. Black has two ways to take it out of there, the more obvious being Nc6-Na5, eliminating at least one of the attacking pieces, unless they are ready to shoot to f7. The other is Qb6 after the c pawn has moved. White has no way to prevent this, so the outcome of the pieces on b3 and c4 must be perfectly timed. Qg3 now unpins the d pawn, and d5 - preventing Nc6 can be played. If d6 as a defence, white moves his dsb and Ra1 goes to b1 or c1, supporting c3 and resting strenght to black's dsb. Ba3, controlling c5 and pinning the e pawn after black castles has its goods, too.
