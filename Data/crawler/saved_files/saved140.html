archduke_piccolo (2206) on 26-Mar-17:
What Kenneth shows here is a well-known tactical motif in the opening known as 'the (pawn) fork trick'. Instead, after 7.Qh5! and now: [a] 7...g6 8.Qd5 wins a piece; [b] 7...d5 8.Nxd5 with a strong game. ++++++++++++++ Having said that, I would regard 7.Nxe4 as only slightly inferior - an inaccuracy, perhaps, rather than an outright mistake, even though Black can play the fork trick. It is still good enough for an advantage: 7.Nxe4 d5 8.Bf4! dxc4 9.Qe2 ... and now, if Black takes the bait: 9...Qxd4?? 10.Rd1! Qa7 (where else to go? Objectively ...QxR is best) 11.Nd6ch with a forced mate. Otherwise 9...Qa5ch(best, probably) 10.c3 e5 (a second fork trick!) 11.Qxc4 Bd7 12.Ng5! with a fun game in prospect, especially for White.

  How can Black defend against the mate? There are five possible defences (a sixth if you count ...Qa5ch) [a] 8...Qe7 9.Nxd6ch Qxd6 10.Qxf7ch with a strong attack; [b] 8...Qd7 9.Ng5 - stacks on the mill at f7 - 9...f6 10.Bf7ch Ke7 11.Be6ch Ke1 12.Nf5ch Ke8 13.Ne3 Bxe6 14.Nxe6 Qc8 15.Nd5 winning. [c] 8...Qc7 (best Q move) 9.Qb3 exd4 10.0-0 11.Nc6 Re1 with a strong attack. [d] 8...Be7 and White can choose for himself whether to play Bxf7ch or Qxf7ch - both should win easily enough. [e] 8...Be6 9.Nxe6 fxe6 10.Bxe6 and White has an overwhelming position.
