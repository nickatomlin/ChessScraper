After being out for a week and coaching Mariann last week, as you know, Jack is now back. And, he wanted me to play against someone who I haven't played against. With that being said, this was an interesting game between me and Bert. For those that are wondering, Bert is a strong player. I asked Jack his apparent rating, and he said that Bert's rating is about 1800 which is why I have it listed as such. Before I even played against this opponent, I knew that Bert was strong, but why? As we know, Jack has annotated many games, and that's the reason why I'm able to annotate a game. But, since it's OTB, an annotation, in this context, isn't written. Instead, Jack shows the students a passed game, and like with an annotation, he goes into a little details about thought process, etc. Unfortunately, it's not, as much as, a regular annotation since we're limited to only 2 hours. But, it is, nontheless, enough to learn, and this is also where he explains things like a GM. Although it's less, when he does explain things and thought process, Jack goes into a lot of details. Some of the games he's shown involves Bert. And, of all of them, this opponent only lost one game. They were so instructive and notable that this is why Jack annotated them. Therefore, I've been wanting to play against this opponent for a while. In addition, given Bert's strength, yes this is why my coach wanted me to play him. The idea being is that it's easier to lose against stronger players, and as easy19 puts it, "Games you win are bad for learning purposes." That's something that Jack has implied saying that I learn by losing games. As of, about 2 weeks ago, he started coming to the chess club. Last week, my goal was achieved, as I, finally, got to play against him. Unfortunately, there was no coaching in our first game. And, as a result, there was no game chat, except for one move. But, I did, however, notate, like last time, the PGN for this annotation. I do this by recording moves on pen and paper. In the second game, which I didn't write down, there was indirect coaching, as Jack got to watch and gave advise. Unfortunately, that one was terrible due to a time crunch. But, this game, without coaching, which is why I just call it OTB Chess Game, as opposed to, OTB Coaching Game was a lot better. Not only that, but this was long and close. So, how did it unfold?

archduke_piccolo (2206) on 19-Jul-15:
This is a fairly unusual line of the Ruy Lopez, but it does get played at Master level from time to time. It is known as 'Bird's Defence' of the Ruy Lopez (the Russians used to call it the Leningrad defence, after the Masters from that city, such as A. Tolush and G. Lissitzin, who used to play it. According to Harry Golombek, the downside of this line is, as you might expect, the fact that Black has moved his only developed piece a second time. But White is best advised against it to eschew the more deliberate build-up of the main lines, and go in for quick development.
