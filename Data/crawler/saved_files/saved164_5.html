 ? - Better was Kd7 or Kd5. White has to be careful himself, here, as he doesn't want his king to be cornered with no escape. Once it becomes clear that Black can stop and win the pawn without the loss of a piece, White could at once take his chances in the infamous king-bishop-knight vs king end game. Though it is a winning endgame, it is not so easy. I have had such endings before - three times, and won all three. But they were all pick-up games, and from positions a deal more favourable to me than would probably have obtained in this game. At this point I was certain we were in for my second ever 100-mover, stretching maybe into 120 moves or more. For the first 100-mover check out: http://gameknot.com/annotation.pl/great-snakes-batman-a-100-mover?gm=18341

archduke_piccolo (2206) on 01-Mar-17:
White could have prolonged the game by 88.a7, forcing me to take the pawn, and then taken his chances in the K+B+N vs K end game: Something like this: 88.a7 Nxa7 89.Kb7 Ke6 90.Ka6 Kd6 91.Ka5 Kc5 92.Ka6 Nb5 93.Kb7 Kd6 Pause here to note that The White King has to be driven to a dark-square corner - one the Black bishop can reach. So White tries to stay clear of both. Black begins by corralling the King, and then driving it where he wants it to go. But he has to watch that the enemy King makes a break TOWARDS the danger corners into order escape into mid-board. That makes it all pretty tricky. To continue: 94.Ka6 Kc6 95.Ka5 Bc5! 96.Ka4 Nd4 97.Ka5 Bd6 98.Ka6 Bb8 99.Ka5 Nc2 100.Ka4 Kc5 (Has White escaped into the open board?) 101.Kb3 Nb4 102 Kc3 Bf4! (You will note that in all this sequence so far, Black has not given check - not once. The object is to rope the enemy king in and pull the coils tighter) 103.Kb3 Be5 104.Ka4 Kc4 105.Ka3 ... (105.Ka5 Bc7ch) 105...Nd3 106.Ka4 Bc7 107.Ka3 Bf4 108.Ka4 Nc5ch! 109.Ka3! ... (Not 109.Ka5?? Bc7#. These unforced checkmate motifs help towards driving the King where Black wants him to go.) 109...Bc1ch 110. Ka2 Kc3 111.Kb1 Nd3 112.Ka1 Kc2 113.Ka2 Nb4ch 114.Ka1 Bb2#.
