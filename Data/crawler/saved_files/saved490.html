Like last time, Joe annotated this game in his club. So, I'm essentially, copying and pasting this annotation from a club post to an annotative format because there is a lot to learn. Unlike last time, however, I will not be showing the entire link because this particular thread had an entire series of games. This was just the last one on his lecture of the Baseman Defense. And, what there is to learn is that even if someone plays an unusual or irregular opening, just like with what I studied in the Owen's and Grunfeld Defenses, you must defend accurately to stay in the game. With that being said, the lecture begins because Joe said, "Ok. Notice anything? Yes, game over in 11 moves! My 11th move pins whites Queen in classic style in line of sight with Whites King."

archduke_piccolo (2206) on 23-Apr-16:
What could White have played here? 11.a3 ... to stop ...Bb4 might have been met by 11...Ne4, unmasking an attack on the h4-knight, and attacking the queen a second time. White pays 12.Qh3 to save both pieces, whence comes 12...Nd4 attacking c2 and a K-R fork. If in response to that White tries castling, then this would happen: 13.0-0 Ne2ch 14.Kh1 Nxf2ch! 15.Rxf2 Qd1ch and mate next. ++++ Had White attempted to avert the pin by castling, no doubt Black would have played a similar line beginning ...Ne4. It is clear that even without this mistake, White was already busted. Maybe 11.Qc4 offered a chance to survive a little longer.

So, this weaker defense was a game of psychology which quickly, in 11 moves overcame whites POTENTIAL opening superiority.
