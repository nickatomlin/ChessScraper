This was an interesting game between sicliandrsgon and one of my students. In my last mentoring session with Erika, she asked me a good question on how to mate, if you have Two Queens and a King against the lonely King. The reason being is that she accepted a draw offer, but my guess is that she was starting to figure out that it was a win. So, using what I know about endgames, including how to mate with two bishops and the knight plus bishop. I told her that if played right (you didn't make a mistake), having two queens was a forced. At first, I based on what I knew about bishop and knight, when I mentioned about locking the monarch in the corner. But, then by figuring out that I was wrong by picturing the position, you can say that I was being corrected by the lord due to having the flashback. I figured that, in that situation, the opponents monarch doesn't have to be in the corner. Instead, with two Queens, the key is to force it on the back rank, so that it can't out. What, my friend, archduke_piccolo said was, "I've just had a look through your latest annotated game with Erika (reachforgoals). At move 4 you have an aside that refers to a game in which she agreed a draw with 2 Queens against a lone King. I had a look at the game in question, and have showed that a move before the end she had at least three ways to checkmate in 2 moves. She also had 2 ways to Stalemate, as well (I show one of them). I think it needs to be pointed out to your student that in that situation, she could have found out for herself what her winning chances were by playing out the game. She had fifty moves available from the promotion of the last pawn, she had absolutely no risk of losing, and so could afford to play on for the win with the draw, as it were, already in her pocket." He also recommended that I point that out to her. When I did, Erika asked me to annotate the game which I promised I would for learning. And, this is it. But, as we go long and walk through it, I'll be coaching and giving her advice since blunders were made on both sides. The idea is to help my student from her mistakes.

archduke_piccolo (2206) on 07-Dec-15:
3...Bd6 has the virtue of getting the bishop out, and over-protects the e-pawn ('over-protection' in chess is more often a good than a bad thing). But there are two down sides: [A] there are better squares for the Bishop, and [B] it interferes with the advance of the d-pawn, which is usually desirable for Black. The consequence of these is that the Bishop will soon have to make another move, which is a loss of time.
