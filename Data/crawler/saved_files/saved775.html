This was a very interesting game between me and Mary. What happened was that two people were absent. So, it was just Mary, Perry, Jack, and I. As he has done it before, I asked Jack if we would be able to play all three of us. The problem, however, is that his glaucoma is becoming worse, so he said that he couldn't play all three of us. Instead, he would play Perry, and I'd play Mary. Since I'm playing against her, I decided to coach her. But, the difference, which I like, is that Jack is there. Therefore, how it works is that I coach and Jack gives advice so that we both learn. When this happens, despite the training that I do, I lose. However, I don't care about winning or losing against a student. All I care is that the student learns, and that's why we're there. In general, when you think of a consultation, it often happens on this website. But, with Jack, you coach or play, and then, he comes and gives advice. With that, it's almost, as good as, an OTB WTW Coaching Game, where he directly coaches you. I found a new person to coach, and believe it or not, it is the second person that I trained who happens to be a student of Jack Stockel. The first one, I directly coached him and won, as you'd expect. But, Jack arrived towards the end of the first game. So, on the second game, he said, "Let me help you", to Perry. Believe it or not, I was glad that he said that. The idea being is that, like with Mary, the person who I coached for a first time is another student of Mr. Stockel, and I am not surprised that, despite the loss, he played quite well on his first game which shows just how good Jack really is. The thing is that even though I realized that I might make a mistake which is even easier to exploit, and when I'd lose, I didn't care. I wasn't there just to win, but rather, I wanted Perry to learn which is why I coached. Now, as we know, Jack is the very reason that I can coach and mentor players the way that I do because, before I saw him, as you know, I was a beginner, and it took me a very long time to improve. So, when he did make a mistake early and I told him it was, I also said that I will let and want Jack to analyze it, as it was the first time in a long while, that I had expertie analysis with someone that I trained. And, he did mention how I would win a Q. So, what it boils down is, the coach right on top of a student, so that way, the student and teacher both learn. Sure enough, that paid off as I made a mistake that lost the game, despite being a Q up, and Jack showed how and what he should play. Now, the last time we had something similar was two weeks ago, and it was during that game. What happened here is something similar. I played and did a little coaching, but Jack witnessed as spectator and gave advice to help Mary. I won against her in the second game, but in this one, I lost due to mistakes, and Jack helped her as I also had things to learn. So, what happened?

archduke_piccolo (2206) on 26-Oct-15:
The sort of thing I mention in my previous note, beginning 12.Kh1!? you can't really coach for. What led me even to consider that move, when there were plenty of other, probably better, options available? It's a pattern thing. Certain features in the position coalesce to form a picture of events: 1.The knight en prise at d4 was threatened WITH CHECK. 2. Having taken on d4, the Black Queen would be unprotected, leading to... 3. Looking for a reasonable discovery by the bishop indicates none is immediately available - but... 4. A knight move to b5 threatens both Queen and a further 'double attack' (fork) at c7. But now... 5. The only move available to deal with both threats exposes the Queen after all to a deadly discovered attack: Nb5 cxb5; Bxb5ch! At that point you would have to assess whether after ...Qxd3 etc, White's game with Queen and more compact pawns is at least equal to Black's 3 minor pieces. Even after 'seeing' this line of play, you might decide: 'Nah. I'd do better with something less exciting, such as 12.Bc4.'
