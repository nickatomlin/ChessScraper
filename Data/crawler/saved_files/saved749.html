damalfi (1742) on 05-Nov-15:
A very good question, more skilled player would respond better than me. In this position (with a solid e4 pawn ready to exchange any d5 or c5 if they want to advance other than to e5) the central pawn bunker (f7, f6, e6, d6) will be the only K shield, the Bdsb will go uncontested to h6, preventing white to castle queenside, the g column will be semiopen, hitting the kinside castling. On the other side, white will have plenty of attack on the Qside, specially if black doesn't play a6 soon, and the central black pawns as an easy target (Qd2, o-o-o, Nd-b5...). But hey: black doesn't have to take with g7: he can also go (BxN,) QxBf6 and it's another game. Normally, high-rated players don't play BxN (at least, not so soon: maybe later), so I guess theory finds it premature - if not bad.
kenneth_fiore_3 (2113) on 06-Nov-15:
Doubled pawns have been discussed since the beginnings of chess. The answer is usually that it depends on what you get for allowing them. Ex. if you double my pawns in an open position and give me the two Bishops, which you don't have I probably like that. If the doubled pawns are going to be on a half=open file and easily blockaded or attacked by you, I'd find that less likeable.

I don't know current theory, but when I was active this was considered the best. I used to play Keres's move 7.Qd3, but that was in the expectation that no one knew the theory.
damalfi (1742) on 05-Nov-15:
Attack with the pawns (and pieces) on the kingside, once the WK is "shielded" on the Qside. In fact, this is risky, since black has the semiopen g file and his own pawn's storm to play (a6, b5, b4...). The soundness of both attacks makes this one of the most played lines of all times, I'd say. Who will break into the opponent's fortress the quicker? There is no simple answer to that question: all this was overanalyzed long ago, and now it is very dangerous to play against a well rated player if not deeply (as possible, because it is an endless taks) studied! Good to "test" your opponent's preparation and tactic ability. The most important thing - for both sides - is to calculate the tempoes he needs to make his attack "uningnorable" - that is: it forces the opponent to defend, spending his tempoes in this instead of attacking you. This is true for both sides! None of them have quick attack: they have to prepare it in more than a move. So, calculating becomes very hard.
