A Positional Zenmaster
R. Fischer vs. J. Bolbochan
Annotated by: teardrop34 (2191)
Chess opening: Sicilian (B90), Najdorf, Adams attack
Interactive ? Show all comments ? All annotated games ? View chessboard as:

"Tactics flow from a superior position." One of the many great quotes from the late great Bobby Fischer. What does it mean though? Many players aren't really sure of what positional chess actually is. Ever played a game where you made no tactical blunders but your opponent's pieces seemed to control the board, you had no good squares to choose from and no weaknesses to attack? That is positional chess. Good tactical ability will get you far but to reach the heights, to eventually become a titled player, a deep understanding of positional chess is essential. This game is one from Fischer's era that show's his positional mastery. One GM claimed that he was so good that Fischer "can throw the pieces up in the air and they land on the correct square!" I don't know about all of that, but Fischer was definitely one of a kind and is definitely missed. But enough about that, onto the game already.

 What is Fischer doing now? Why is his attack so slow if his position is so good? He's a zen master as I said before, he's biding his time, weakening black's position even more. A problem that lower rated players have is that they rush the play and don't consider the build up. They do not consider the beauty of the build up. Just like times of war, you have to have strategies to destroy an enemy, not a strategy, but strategies in case one fails.
