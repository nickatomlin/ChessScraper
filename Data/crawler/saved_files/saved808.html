No, it's not a pin, but rather a fork since two pieces are attacked by one. A pin is if you made it so that one piece couldn't move, so that it's pinned. It either can't move because it would be illegal (a direct pin), or because it would lose something worth more (an indirect pin) An example of a pin, happened in this position.

White's Knight was on d2, but he played Nc4. Now, as you notice, black cannot play, for example, ...Qe6 as that would be an illegal move. In other words, it cannot move off of the d file, as it would be against the rules of chess since black would place himself. Therefore, it is a pin since black's Queen is confined to the d file. Consequently, as you might imagine, black will lose his Queen. Now, there are plenty of examples, however, of a pin, but I decided to give you one of the most extremes examples I could think of, so that, it will become second nature (you become familiar and understand this quickly) as a pin and fork are very important chess terms and, in general, should not get mixed up. I know because it took me a long time, about 6 months to remember these terms, but from examples like this, I, finally, remembered, once and for all and now teach this, as I am now.
