I am annotating this to highlight the importance of king safety, and the consequences that can occur when one side neglects to pay attention to this aspect of the game. Here, white plays a solid opening, developing nicely, but forgot the most important part of the opening: protect the king! See if you as the reader can identify exactly where white went wrong. Also, let's see where white was doing well.

archduke_piccolo (2206) on 27-Jan-17:
I know this is late in the day, but I hope you get to read this nairobigal. In my view you have to start with small steps. I infer that by 'small pieces' you mean pawns, and by 'big pieces' you mean the ones lined up on the back rank at the start of the game. Generally we tend to distinguish them as 'pawns' and 'pieces' . The queen and rooks are called 'major' pieces (sometimes for the sake of variety, heavy pieces); and the knights and bishops are minor pieces. On account of his unique status, the King is very rarely referred to collectively among the other pieces. ++++++ I suggest that in the first half-dozen moves of the game, you aim to move no more than 2 pawns (neither of them rook pawns), get 3 minor pieces off the back rank and into the game, and castle King-side. Divert from this programme only if forced to do so in order to avert loss of material or checkmate OR, if you see a concrete opportunity to gain material or checkmate. It might be an idea to learn the Scholar's Mate as well. There are mean people out there who will try it on you. This should set you up with an active game with good chances.
