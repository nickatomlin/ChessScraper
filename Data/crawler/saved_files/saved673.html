 Here, we have quite a popular position, the Sicilian Dragon. White aims to play g4, h4, and h5 at some point to rip open Black's kingside structure, while Black aims to push his a- and b-pawns down the queenside to tear apart White's structure on that side. True to the Sicilian spirit, this game showcases both sides claiming space on their respective flanks of the board, with both sides engaging in a sharp attack. One misstep can result in a hopeless position in an opening as sharp as this.

 Here, we have the Yugoslav Attack. Both sides have effectively committed to a full-out Sicilian battle with each side's pieces constantly at the other king's throat. In this type of opening, whoever falters first signs their own death sentence. White aims to exchange his e3 bishop for Black's g7 bishop at some point, and then he plans to march his g- and h-pawns up the board and open up the h-file. Should Black sit back and allow this to happen, he will surely lose. However, Black has some threats of his own. He plans to target the loose a2 pawn and take advantage of the fact that his pieces are very active on the queenside.
