archduke_piccolo (2206) on 28-Jun-16:
6...b6 looks out of place in the position Black has set up. In my view, ...d6 or ...d5 would have been better choices. 6...h6 also comes into consideration, since the g5-bishop is there to be hit (Will White exchange on f6, or retire the bishop? Black would have to accept the loss of a pawn, and possibly the scrambling of his structure as well: for example: 7.Nxe5 Qe8!? (or 7...h6 or ...Bb7) 8.Bxf6 gxf6 9.Nxc6 dxc6 10.d4 Bd6 11.Nd2, say - a considerable advantage to White.

archduke_piccolo (2206) on 28-Jun-16:
Actually, White would have had to weigh the consequences of 9...Nxe4 when playing this advance. It is the sort of thing that crops up quite often from bishops standing on g5 (or g4) pinning a knight, say. It is this: 9.e4 Nxe4!? 10.Bxe7 Nxc3 11.Bxd8 Nxd1 12.Bxc7 Nxb2 13.Bxe5 Nxf5!? 14.Bxb7 Rb8 with a roughly level game. This kind of single handed slaughter by a bishop on one side and a knight on the other crops up surprisingly often. In this case, Black doesn't not come out with the pawn extra, but in very similar positions he does. And of course, on other occasions, some feature of the position rules the motif out of court: unplayable.
